## ⚠️ CRITICAL FINDING: Wet Versterking Participatie

**This changes your pitch.**

The Wet Versterking Participatie op decentraal niveau is real and did take effect on 1 January 2025 ✓. But it does NOT do what your current docs claim.

### What the law actually requires

Municipalities must replace their old "inspraakverordening" (consultation ordinance) with a new "participatieverordening" by **1 January 2027** (2-year transition). This ordinance must specify how residents participate in policy preparation, implementation, and evaluation. It also introduces the "uitdaagrecht" (challenge right) — residents can propose taking over execution of municipal tasks.

### What the law does NOT require

- Digital platforms of any kind
- Volunteer coordination tools
- Participation tracking software
- Reporting dashboards

The law is about **governance structure** (how to involve citizens in policy-making), not about **volunteer coordination** (matching people to practical tasks). These are legally separate categories.

### What this means for KarmaKaart

Your current pitch says: "Per 1 januari 2025 is de Wet Versterking Participatie van kracht — alle 342 gemeenten moeten aantonen hoe ze burgers betrekken." This is technically true but misleading. The law doesn't require a platform like yours.

**The honest reframe:** "Gemeenten moeten een participatieverordening opstellen en aantonen dat burgers betrokken worden. KarmaKaart levert de data die dat bewijs levert — meetbare participatie per wijk, per maand, real-time."

You're not selling compliance. You're selling **evidence of impact**. That's still valuable, but it's a different conversation.

### No penalties found

No documented sanctions for municipalities that don't comply by 2027. This weakens the urgency argument further. The pressure is reputational and political, not legal.

## Competitive Landscape

### NLvoorelkaar — The incumbent

The largest Dutch volunteer platform. 200,000+ participants, 15,000+ community organisations, 19 million hours realised. Serves 60+ municipalities with white-label local platforms (e.g., Zorgzaam010 in Rotterdam, VrijwilligersTilburg in Tilburg).

**Business model:** Social enterprise, white-label platforms for municipalities. Pricing not publicly available.

**What they do well:** Massive installed base, network effects, local brand recognition via municipality partnerships.

**What they DON'T do (your gap):** No map-based discovery, no check-in/validation (QR or GPS), no micro-volunteering time filters, no real-time municipal dashboards with ROI metrics. They are a matching platform — volunteer sees listing, applies, organisation responds. That's a fundamentally different flow from "open map → pick → go → check in."

Sources: https://tracxn.com/d/companies/nlvoorelkaar/, https://zakelijk.nlvoorelkaar.nl/vrijwilligerscentrales/

### Deedmob — Corporate volunteering focus

12,500+ organisations, 75,000+ first-time volunteers. Primary revenue from corporate clients (companies pay for employee volunteering programs). Municipal work exists but is secondary.

**Municipal partnerships:** Amsterdam (via Business Involved), Vrijwilligerspunt Dalfsen. Provided free platforms during COVID.

**Pricing:** Not publicly available. Enterprise model, likely €10k+/year for corporate clients.

**Why they're not your direct competitor:** Their money comes from corporates, not municipalities. Different buyer, different sales cycle.

Sources: https://www.deedmob.com/about/about-deedmob, https://www.deedmob.com/customer-cases/business-involved

### OpenStad — Open source participation (not volunteering)

Open source platform for citizen participation: voting, participatory budgeting, plan submission. NOT a volunteer platform. Used by 50+ municipalities and 3 provinces.

**Cost:** €3,500-€6,500 implementation, custom hosting fees via Draad.

**Why it matters:** Supported by VNG, which gives it credibility. But it does participation (policy co-creation), not volunteering. If a municipality says "we already have OpenStad," your answer is "great — we complement that, we don't replace it."

Sources: https://openstad.org/wat-het-kost, https://draad.nl/dienst/openstad/

### WeHelpen — Informal care matching

Free online platform for informal caregivers and volunteers, cooperative model (since 2012, Breda). Focus on health care and neighbourhood care. Pricing not publicly available.

**Difference:** Focus on informal caregiving networks (mantelzorg), not micro-volunteering tasks.

Source: https://www.age-platform.eu/dutch-online-platform-for-mutual-support-and-solidarity/

### Volunteerly — Modern, low-barrier

Modern platform with emphasis on simplicity: neighbour help, workshops, events, activity calendars. Works with municipalities. Scale and pricing unknown.

**Worth watching:** Their positioning around "low barrier" and modern UX is closest to KarmaKaart's philosophy.

Source: https://www.volunteerly.nl/

### VWC-BUUV (Haarlem)

Local platform for Haarlem and Bloemendaal. 10,000+ matches/year, 300+ partner organisations. Not a national player.

Source: https://www.vwc-buuv.nl/

### What NO ONE does (your actual gap)

After researching all major Dutch platforms, none of them offer:

1. **Map-first discovery** with geographic proximity as the primary interface
2. **Check-in/check-out validation** (QR code or GPS)
3. **Micro-volunteering with time filters** (1 hour / 2 hours / half day)
4. **Real-time municipal dashboards** with ROI calculation (hours × €15)
5. **5-minute-to-first-activity flow** (sign up → map → pick → do)

This is genuine differentiation. The question is whether municipalities will pay for it.

## VMCA Almere

### What VMCA is

Full name: Vrijwilligers en Mantelzorg Centrale Almere. Independent foundation (stichting), not part of the municipality. Exists 40+ years. Coordinates volunteers and informal caregivers in Almere.

### Funding

VMCA operates on a Results-Based Subsidy Distribution (RSV) model with Gemeente Almere. The claim "volledig gesubsidieerd door gemeente" is likely true but could not be fully verified via public sources. VMCA publishes annual reports at https://vmca-radar.nl/ (PDFs not accessible via web search).

Source: https://www.vmca.nl/over-ons, https://www.vrijwilligersaanzet.nl/organiseren-van-vrijwilligers/financiering-van-vrijwilligerswerk/

### Software they use

**Primary system: Qsuite** (by Novire) — SaaS web application for registering volunteers, informal caregivers, and supported persons. VMCA migrated from 8 separate systems to Qsuite in 2018. Has security certification (Stichting SoftwareBorg, IT-notaris).

**Secondary system: RAET** — online HR/personnel registration.

**Public-facing: linked to NLvoorelkaar** — their website connects to the national NLvoorelkaar platform for volunteer matching.

Source: VMCA privacy statement and annual reports

### What this means for KarmaKaart

VMCA is a potential ketenpartner (they would place markers on the map), NOT a competitor. They already use digital tools (Qsuite) so they understand software workflows. Their frustration points likely include: demonstrating impact to the municipality, attracting new (younger) volunteers, and reducing administrative overhead.

When you meet with VMCA, ask:
1. How much time do you spend on coordination that could be automated?
2. How do you currently report impact to Gemeente Almere?
3. What does your RSV agreement look like — what metrics must you hit?
4. Would you place your activities as markers on a platform if it brought you more volunteers?

## How vrijwilligerscentrales work nationally

Almost every Dutch municipality has a vrijwilligerscentrale. They are typically independent foundations, funded by municipal subsidies. They match volunteers to organisations, provide advice, and handle VOG coordination.

**Common pain points across the sector:**

1. Demonstrating impact to the funding municipality (how to prove ROI)
2. Attracting younger volunteers (current base is aging)
3. Competition with "buurtteams" (neighbourhood teams) for coordination role
4. Shrinking budgets vs. growing demand
5. Complex administration (registration, VOG processing, scheduling)

Source: https://www.movisie.nl/artikel/hoe-maken-we-vrijwilligerscentrale-toekomstbestendig

**Common software:** NLvoorelkaar (most common), VrijwilligersDossier, Fenna (Kenniscentrum Vrijwilligers), Qsuite (VMCA Almere specifically).

Source: https://zakelijk.nlvoorelkaar.nl/vrijwilligerscentrales/, https://www.vrijwilligersaanzet.nl/vrijwilligers-software-fenna/

## Updated Strategic Recommendations

### 1. Rewrite your municipality pitch

Don't sell compliance. Sell evidence of impact. Municipalities need to prove that participation works — you give them the data. That's a legitimate value proposition without overstating the law.

### 2. Position as complement to NLvoorelkaar, not replacement

NLvoorelkaar is entrenched in 60+ municipalities. Fighting them is suicide. Instead: "NLvoorelkaar matches. We track what happens after the match. Together, you get the full picture." Some vrijwilligerscentrales might even see you as an upgrade to their existing NLvoorelkaar integration.

### 3. Target the vrijwilligerscentrale as your champion

VMCA-type organisations are your on-the-ground users. They have the municipality's ear, they control the volunteer supply, and they're frustrated with outdated tools. Win the vrijwilligerscentrale → they advocate to the municipality → municipality buys.

### 4. Fix the BZK Innovatiebudget timeline

The 2026 round (deadline March 31, 2026) is your window — not the 2025 round you reference. This actually gives you MORE time to prepare a strong application with a pilot municipality as consortium lead.
